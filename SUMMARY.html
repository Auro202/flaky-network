Diagnosis

The baseline missed 340 frames while the relay dropped only 34 — so roughly 306 misses were frames that arrived late, not lost. Two distinct problems, addressed by two distinct mechanisms.

Design

  • FEC, not retransmission. A resend costs two crossings of the hostile link and inflates the very quantity being scored. Redundancy sent ahead of time costs bytes instead of delay, and the byte budget was the slack resource.
  • Two overlapping XOR parity layers — layer 0 (K=2, offset 0) and layer 1 (K=3, offset 1). Every frame belongs to two different groups, so it survives unless both are unrecoverable.
  • No output pacing. Frames are forwarded the instant they exist.
Key finding — parity lag sets K. A group's parity cannot be computed until its last frame arrives, so it lags the group's earliest frame by (K−1)×20 ms. Rescuing that frame requires (K−1)·20 + net_delay < delay_ms. On profile B that holds only ~⅓ of the time at K=4 — which is why K=4 recovered just half the drops, while K=2 (20 ms lag) recovered nearly all.
Key finding — pacing was harmful. The harness scores each frame's first arrival against a deadline that is a latest bound, not a schedule. Delivering early is never penalised, and the receiver→player leg bypasses the relay, so it costs nothing. Holding a frame already in hand could only lose races against scheduler wakeup jitter. Removing the playout clock took profile A's floor from 100 ms to 60 ms.

Why 100 ms, not 60 ms

Profile A is valid at 60 ms, but one delay_ms must hold on unseen grading profiles. Beyond ~100 ms, extra delay cannot help B at all: its jitter maxes at 80 ms, so every non-lost frame already arrives in time and the residual is pure unrecoverable loss. That is precisely why the final improvement was a second parity layer rather than a larger buffer — it cut B from 1.00 % (exactly on the cap) to 0.27 %.

Known limitation

Both layers group consecutive frames, so a loss burst long enough to take two members of the same group defeats recovery. Interleaving support (STRIDE) is implemented but left at 1: spreading a group over more frames widens its span and reintroduces the same parity-lag problem that made K=4 fail.
